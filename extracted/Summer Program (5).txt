Rocket Fundamentals
1.1. What is a Reusable Launch Vehicle (RLV)?
One should have a precise understanding of what an RLV is, both conceptually and structurally, before
writing any relevant equations. A reusable launch vehicle (RLV) is a launch system engineered to deliver
payloads to orbit while returning substantially intact to Earth, allowing for recovery, refurbishment, and
multiple subsequent flights to achieve cost reductions over single-use alternatives — distinguishing it
from expendable launch vehicles that are discarded after one use, and prioritizing lifecycle cost efficiency
through hardware reuse rather than optimizing for minimal mass in disposable components.
As a result, core engineering principles of RLVs centre on maintaining structural integrity across multiple
stress cycles, including the extreme loads from ascent, orbital insertion, atmospheric reentry, and landing,
which necessitate robust materials and higher safety margins compared to expendable systems. This is
the foundational tension that runs through every day of this course: reusability saves money over time
but adds mass and complexity up front.
1.2 Functional Grouping of RLV Components
The rationale for functional grouping is as follows:
● Changing one component affects primarily its functional group, limiting redesign ripple effects.
● Failures propagate within a functional group, which informs reliability modelling (Day 8).
● New functions introduced by reusability map cleanly onto new functional groups, making the cost
of reuse visible and quantifiable.
A complete reusable rocket contains approximately ~70 essential components, of which ~35 are required
by any rocket (expendable or reusable), and ~35 are added exclusively to support reusability. This implies
that reuse nearly doubles the component count, and hence significantly increases the complexity of the
rocket. In particular, all thermal protection systems are exclusively reusability-driven. The four functional
groups are laid out below.

1.3 Reference Vehicle A: SpaceX Falcon 9
The Falcon 9 serves as a design reference throughout this course because its architecture closely mirrors
what the team is designing: a two-stage, LOX/RP-1, vertically landing reusable vehicle.
The Falcon 9 is composed of four main components: the first stage rocket, the interstage, the second
stage rocket, and the payload fairing. The first stage consists of nine Merlin gimballed engines and
aluminium-lithium alloy tanks containing high-density chilled LOX and cooled RP-1 propellant.
The first stage engines are arranged in a circular configuration called the "Octaweb" pattern, with eight
engines surrounding a centre engine. This pattern reduces the booster's length and weight, and simplifies
the rocket's design and assembly.
The engines perform a "boostback burn" to bring trajectory toward the landing site, deploy grid fins, an
"entry burn" to slow down the first stage, aerodynamic guidance, and then a "landing burn" for a precise
landing as it deploys landing legs and lands upright.
SpaceX designs Block 5 for up to 15 flights with minimal refurbishment, though a few boosters have
already hit 18-plus flights thanks to careful handling.
The key lesson from Falcon 9: by late 2014, SpaceX suspended plans to recover and reuse the Falcon 9
second stage; the additional mass of the required heat shield, landing gear, and low-powered landing
engines would incur too great a performance penalty. This is a concrete, historical demonstration of the
reusability mass trade-off that students must apply in their own design.

2.2 The “Tyranny of the Rocket Equation”
The exponential relationship between mass ratio and delta-v creates what aerospace engineers call "the
tyranny of the rocket equation." A single-stage rocket with chemical propulsion (Isp ≈ 300–450 s)
attempting to reach low Earth orbit (Δv ≈ 9,400 m/s accounting for all losses) would require a mass ratio of
exp(9400/4000) ≈ 10.9.
At a mass ratio of 20 with LOX/RP-1, 95% of the launch mass must be propellant, leaving only 5% for
structure, engines, and payload. In practice, structural mass alone consumes 5 to 8% of initial mass,
making single-stage orbit impossible with chemical propulsion. Staging discards heavy empty tanks and
engines mid-flight, allowing subsequent stages to achieve their portion of the ΔV budget with a much
more favourable mass ratio.
A concrete sensitivity illustration: a 10% improvement in Isp (300 s → 330 s) for the same delta-v allows
payload to increase by roughly 64% — an exponential leverage that is why engine designers chase every
second of Isp.
(https://www.kmi.space/columns/tyranny-of-the-rocket-equation)

2.3 Velocity Budget
Real missions must account for gravity losses (typically 1,500–2,000 m/s for Earth launch), atmospheric
drag (100–300 m/s), and steering losses (50–150 m/s), so the ideal Tsiolkovsky equation is modified by
these real-world additions.
The complete ΔV budget to LEO is therefore:

Total ΔV budget (LEO) ~9,200–9,700
The equation assumes no gravity or atmospheric drag, so real-world ΔV must be increased by 1,200 to

For instance, the Falcon 9 first stage, utilising RP-1 and liquid oxygen (LOX), achieves a PMF of
approximately 0.94, with a propellant mass of about 411 metric tonnes out of a total stage mass of 439
metric tonnes.
Staging is the most important engineering trick in rocketry which is a direct response to the Tsiolkovsky
equation's tyranny. Instead of one giant rocket, smaller rockets are stacked on top of each other. When the
first stage burns out, it is discarded. The empty tanks and engines become dead weight contributing to mf
and reducing the mass ratio. By jettisoning them, the equation restarts with a much more favourable mass
ratio for the next stage.
Each stage is enlarged so that it generates ΔV equal to the optimal velocity gain plus the losses occurring
during its flight — and each stage accounts for the increased mass of the stages on top of it. This is the
cascade logic of multi-stage sizing: always work from the top stage downward.

2.6 The Core Trade-off: Reusability Mass Penalty
Reusability is not "free." Every recovery system (landing legs, grid fins, thermal protection, reserved
propellant, RCS thrusters) consumes mass that would otherwise be payload or structure:
The weight penalty for the landing gear was estimated by reverse-engineering SpaceX's Falcon 9. To
consider uncertainties in the mass estimates, neglected components, and neglected additional loads
(e.g., due to gusts), additional mass margins of 10% for the upper stage and 15% for the first stage are
applied to reflect more substantial uncertainties about the landing gear structures.
When boosters are intended to be expended, the landing legs and grid fins are omitted from the vehicle.
The landing legs are made of carbon fibre with an aluminium honeycomb structure.
This is why: two-stage-to-orbit with reuse dominates — Falcon 9 Stage 1 is reusable, Stage 2 is
expendable, and the economics work out. Starship is attempting full two-stage reuse, which is the closest

Propulsion System (Engine Selection) 13 July 2026 (Day 3)
Propulsion System
1.1. What is a propulsion system?
The propulsion system is the heart of the rocket. It is the system that produces the thrust needed to lift
the rocket off the ground, accelerate it through the atmosphere, and propel it into orbit. It includes all the
components that work together to burn propellant and expel exhaust gases at high speed – creating the
reaction force (thrust) that pushes the rocket forward (Newton's Third Law). [1]
[1] (https://www1.grc.nasa.gov/beginners-guide-to-aeronautics/rocket-propulsion/)
1.2 Main types or examples of the propulsion system
Propulsion systems are broadly classified by propellant state and thrust generation method. Liquid
propulsion stores fuel and oxidizer separately, pumping them into a combustion chamber for controlled,
throttleable, and restartable thrust—ideal for reusable rockets like Falcon 9 (Merlin, RP-1/LOX) and
Starship (Raptor, methane/LOX). Solid propulsion uses a pre-mixed propellant cast as a solid grain; once
ignited, it cannot be throttled or shut down, offering simplicity and high thrust but low performance, as
seen in the Space Shuttle SRBs. Hybrid propulsion combines a solid fuel with a liquid oxidizer, providing
some throttle control with simpler storage, though it remains experimental. Electric propulsion (e.g., ion
thrusters) accelerates ions electrically for extremely high efficiency but very low thrust, used only for
deep-space station-keeping, not launch. For reusable launch vehicles, liquid propulsion is the clear
choice due to its controllability, restart capability, and compatibility with multiple flight cycles. [2]
[2] (https://www.grc.nasa.gov/WWW/K-12/rocket/rktengine.html)

weight and volume, solid propulsion is the most compact and lightweight because of its high-density
propellant, making it ideal for booster stages where space is limited. Liquid propulsion requires larger
tanks, adding structural mass and volume, while hybrid falls between the two. Electric propulsion is
extremely light in terms of propellant mass but requires heavy solar panels and power conditioning
equipment, making it less practical for launch vehicles. For reusability, liquid propulsion scores highest
due to its throttleability, restart capability, and inspectability, making it the clear choice for reusable
rockets. Solid propulsion scores lowest because its grain degrades with each cycle and cannot be
throttled or restarted. Hybrid offers moderate reusability potential but remains unproven, while electric
propulsion offers high reusability but is unsuitable for launch due to its extremely low thrust. Based on this
comparison, liquid and solid propulsion emerge as the most viable options for primary launch
applications, with liquid being the superior choice for reusable systems due to its controllability and
long-term economic benefits.
1.4 Narrowing down the selection of the propulsion system
.
Our propulsion system selection is driven by three critical factors: cost, weight and volume, and
reusability. These constraints narrow our choice to either solid or liquid propulsion systems, as hybrid
and electric options are either too immature or unsuitable for primary launch applications.
Cost is the first major consideration. Solid propulsion systems are generally cheaper to develop and
manufacture due to their simpler design and fewer moving parts. However, this upfront cost advantage
fades when considering operational expenses—solid motors are single-use and must be completely
rebuilt after each flight, whereas liquid engines can be refurbished and reused. For our reusable rocket,
the long-term economic benefit of liquid propulsion outweighs its higher initial investment. [3]
Weight and volume also play a decisive role. Solid propellants have higher density, allowing more
propellant mass in a smaller volume. This compactness is advantageous for booster stages where thrust
is critical. Liquid propellants—especially cryogenic ones like liquid hydrogen—have much lower density,
requiring larger and heavier tanks. This increases structural mass and drag, reducing overall efficiency.
Reusability is the most decisive factor for our project. Solid motors cannot be throttled or restarted,
making powered vertical landing impossible. Additionally, the grain degrades with each thermal and
pressure cycle, and cannot be easily inspected or re-qualified without extensive disassembly. Liquid
engines, in contrast, offer deep throttling, multiple restarts, and straightforward health monitoring. Their
ability to perform precision landing burns makes liquid propulsion the only viable choice for a reusable
launch vehicle.
For these reasons—we select liquid propulsion for our rocket design.

1.6 Types of liquid rocket propulsion systems
.
LOX/Kerosene (液氧煤油)
LOX/Kerosene is a well-established liquid propellant combination, using liquid oxygen (LOX) as the
oxidizer and refined kerosene (RP-1) as the fuel. Kerosene has a high density, allowing for relatively
compact propellant tanks, which helps reduce the overall size and structural mass of the rocket. The
specific impulse of this combination ranges from approximately 280 to 310 seconds at sea level and 340
to 360 seconds in vacuum, offering moderate performance. Its main advantages include high density,
compact tank volume, relatively simple ground handling, and stable storage at room temperature.
However, kerosene combustion produces soot and carbon deposits (coking), which can clog engine
cooling channels and injectors, increasing maintenance and refurbishment complexity – a notable
drawback for reusable rockets. Furthermore, its specific impulse is lower than that of methane and
hydrogen combinations, representing a performance compromise. Typical LOX/Kerosene engines
include SpaceX's Merlin engine (used on Falcon 9) and Russia's RD-180 engine (used on Atlas V). [6]
[6]: (https://www.spaceflightinsider.com/engineering/propulsion/)
LOX/Methane (液氧甲烷)
LOX/Methane is a more recent propellant combination that has gained significant attention in modern
reusable rocket design. Methane (CH₄) offers a good balance between performance and practicality,
delivering a specific impulse of approximately 300 to 330 seconds at sea level and 350 to 370 seconds in
vacuum – notably higher than kerosene but lower than hydrogen. Its key advantages lie in its excellent
compatibility with reusability: methane combustion produces minimal soot or coking, which greatly
simplifies engine inspection and refurbishment between flights. Additionally, methane can be produced
on Mars using local resources (in-situ resource utilization), making it an attractive choice for future
deep-space missions. Methane is also cryogenic, requiring careful thermal management, but its boiling
point is higher than hydrogen's, making it somewhat easier to handle. [7]
[7]: (https://www.blueorigin.com/engines/be-4)
LOX/LH2 (液氧液氢)
LOX/LH2 is the highest-performance liquid propellant combination commonly used in rocketry, using
liquid oxygen as the oxidizer and liquid hydrogen (LH₂) as the fuel. It delivers an outstanding specific
impulse of approximately 350 to 370 seconds at sea level and 420 to 450 seconds in vacuum – the

highest among chemical propellants – making it the preferred choice for upper stages where efficiency is
paramount. However, this performance comes with significant challenges: liquid hydrogen has an
extremely low density (approximately 0.07 g/cm³), requiring very large and heavy tanks that increase
structural mass and aerodynamic drag. It is also deeply cryogenic, with a boiling point of -253°C,
demanding advanced thermal insulation and careful handling. Hydrogen is highly volatile and prone to
leakage due to its small molecular size. Despite these drawbacks, LOX/LH2 remains the gold standard
for high-energy upper stages, with examples including the RL10 engine (used on Centaur and Delta IV
upper stages) and the J-2 engine (used on the Saturn V's upper stages). [8] [9]

In conclusion, we select LOX/Kerosene as our propulsion system. While methane offers cleaner

practical advantages for our mission. Methane's lower density would require larger tanks, increasing
structural mass and overall vehicle size, while hydrogen's extreme cost, massive tank volume, and
hazardous handling make it impractical for a first-stage booster. With a density significantly higher than
both, kerosene allows for compact propellant tanks, reducing fuselage diameter, structural mass, and
aerodynamic drag – directly translating to lower manufacturing cost and a more manageable vehicle size.
Our target payload of 20,000 kg to SSO does not demand hydrogen's extreme efficiency, and the coking
issue of kerosene, while real, is manageable. Modern engine designs and post-flight cleaning procedures,
successfully demonstrated by Falcon 9's Merlin engines over multiple reuses, have proven that coking
can be effectively addressed. Given our emphasis on cost-effectiveness, structural simplicity, and proven
technology, LOX/Kerosene provides the optimal balance of performance, density, and operational
maturity for our first-stage booster..
1.8 Final Verdict
.
After evaluating all three liquid propellant combinations against our mission requirements – payload
capacity, reusability, cost, and structural simplicity – LOX/Kerosene emerges as the optimal choice.
Methane's cleaner combustion and hydrogen's superior specific impulse are compelling, but both come
with significant trade-offs: methane requires larger, heavier tanks that increase structural mass and drag,
while hydrogen demands impractically large tanks, extreme cryogenic handling, and prohibitive costs
that make it unsuitable for a first-stage booster. Kerosene offers the best balance of density, thrust, and
operational maturity, with manageable trade-offs successfully addressed in existing reusable systems like
Falcon 9's Merlin engines, which have demonstrated multiple reuses with effective post-flight cleaning.
For our mission – delivering 20,000 kg to SSO with a reusable first stage – the practical advantages of
compact tanks, lower structural mass, reduced drag, and proven technology outweigh the coking
drawback. Therefore, we confidently select LOX/Kerosene as the propulsion system for our reusable
launch vehicle.

2.1 Engine Cycle Trade Study
Cycle A: Gas Generator (GG) — "Open Cycle"
In the gas generator cycle, a small amount of fuel and oxidizer is burned in a preburner, which drives a
turbine to pump more fuel and oxidizer into the main combustion chamber. The fuel and oxidizer are
then burned in the main combustion chamber, producing high-pressure exhaust gases that exit through
the nozzle to provide thrust.
The critical characteristic: the turbine exhaust is dumped overboard and never reaches the main nozzle.
This cycle is inherently somewhat lossy, in that the turbine gas is not fully utilized in the main combustor.
On the other hand, the power control is relatively straightforward, and there is little interaction of the feed
system with the rest of the rocket.

The advantage of the staged combustion cycle is that all of the engine cycles' gases and heat go
through the combustion chamber, and overall efficiency essentially suffers no pumping losses at all.
This combustion cycle is often called 'closed cycle' since the cycle is closed as all propellant products go
through the chamber, as opposed to open cycle which dumps the turbopump driving gases, representing
a few percent of loss. Another very significant advantage that staged combustion gives is an abundance
of power which permits very high chamber pressures. Very high chamber pressures mean high
expansion ratio nozzles can be used, whilst still giving ambient pressures at takeoff. These nozzles give far
better efficiencies at low altitude.
However, the disadvantages of this cycle are harsh turbine conditions, that more exotic plumbing is
required to carry the hot gases, and that a very complicated feedback and control design is necessary. In
particular, running the full oxidizer stream through both a pre-combustor and main-combustor chamber
(oxidizer-rich staged combustion) produces extremely corrosive gases.

2.3 Long March 10B
Long March 10B is the most important new reference because it demonstrates a different solution to
first-stage recovery. Reports following its 10 July 2026 debut state that the vehicle placed its payload into
orbit and recovered its first stage at sea using a net/cable capture system mounted on a recovery vessel
[3]. NASASpaceFlight describes Long March 10B as a two-stage partially reusable rocket with seven
YF-100K kerolox first-stage engines and a single YF-219 methane/LOX gas-generator upper-stage
engine; the same source reports approximately 8,750 kN of lift-off thrust and 16,000 kg payload capability
to LEO in reusable configuration [4].
The most relevant feature is not the propellant combination, but the recovery architecture. Unlike Falcon
9, which carries deployable landing legs, Long March 10B reportedly shifts part of the recovery hardware
from the vehicle to the recovery vessel. The booster uses grid fins and powered descent, then engages a
ship-mounted capture system rather than standing on full onboard legs [3]. This changes the
mass-budget trade: landing-energy absorption and final support are no longer entirely flight hardware. In
principle, this can reduce reusable-stage dry mass and increase payload or mass margin. It also
introduces new risks, including vessel complexity, catch-load concentration, sea-state constraints, and
the need to inspect catch interfaces after each flight.
For the present report, Long March 10B motivates the selected design direction: a hybrid ocean
catch-compatible booster. The project does not duplicate the Long March 10B configuration directly. The
baseline retains LOX/RP-1 in both stages because the propulsion trade study selected kerolox for
density, tank compactness, and operational maturity. It also avoids assuming Long March 10B’s
unpublished dry masses or refurbishment cost. Net/cable recovery is treated as an emerging technology
with one reported orbital-class recovery demonstration rather than as a fully mature operational system.

2.4 Ocean Recovery Operations
The target orbit is SSO, which commonly implies high-inclination or polar-like launch trajectories. For
such missions, downrange ocean recovery is operationally more relevant than return-to-launch-site
recovery because the booster naturally travels downrange and because returning to the launch site
generally requires additional boostback propellant.
Recent Falcon 9 operations reinforce this point. Spaceflight Now reported that Falcon 9 booster B1097
landed on the drone ship Of Course I Still Love You in the Pacific Ocean after the Transporter-17 rideshare
mission from Vandenberg Space Force Base on 7 July 2026 [5]. Spaceflight Now also reported that
another Falcon 9 mission on 13 July 2026 landed booster B1093 on the same Pacific drone ship and
identified the landing as SpaceX’s 637th booster landing to date [6].
These operational examples do not determine the exact mass budget, but they support the selection of
downrange ocean recovery as the project’s baseline recovery environment. The baseline architecture
therefore assumes that the first stage will be recovered by a sea-based platform rather than by return to
the launch site. This assumption is consistent with the project’s SSO mission and permits a meaningful
comparison between Falcon-style drone-ship landing and Long-March-10B-inspired catch recovery.

3. Structural and Material Developments
3.1 Material Zoning Strategy
The material strategy is based on selective placement, not universal use of high-cost materials. A
reusable launch vehicle contains regions with fundamentally different load cases: cryogenic pressure
vessels, axial compression shells, thrust-transfer structures, interstage bending members, hot engine-bay
components, descent-control surfaces, and recovery-interface hardware. Applying one material family
across the full vehicle would be structurally inefficient and difficult to inspect after recovery.
The baseline strategy is therefore a zoned structure. Metallic aluminum-lithium tanks remain the primary
pressure-bearing structure; composite materials are used where aerodynamic stiffness and low mass
dominate; titanium, maraging steel, nickel superalloys, and high-temperature ceramics are reserved for
localized thermal or concentrated-load regions. This approach is consistent with launch-vehicle structural
practice, where the material choice is coupled to manufacturability, inspection, and load-path clarity
rather than maximum material strength alone. NASA launch-vehicle structure guidance notes that
propellant tanks are commonly stiffened metal shells using integrally machined isogrid or orthogrid
patterns, and that manufacturability is as important as intrinsic material properties [9].

3.2 Sensor-Enabled Reuse
A reusable booster must be recovered and then certified for another flight. Visual inspection alone is
insufficient because critical damage may occur inside composite layers, welded joints, tank walls, grid-fin
roots, thrust structures, or recovery fittings. The architecture therefore includes a sensor-enabled reuse
passport: a flight-by-flight digital record of structural strain, thermal exposure, acceleration, catch loads,
and selected pressure-cycle data.
Fiber Bragg Grating (FBG) sensors are the most relevant technology for this concept. ESA work on
fiber-optic sensing in space structures identifies FBG sensors as promising for reusable cryogenic tank
and intertank monitoring because they can be multiplexed along a single optical fiber, embedded or
surface-mounted, and used for strain and temperature mapping [10]. A later study on composite
propellant tanks states that embedded FBG networks can contribute to operations and condition

===== PAGE 37 =====
Mass Budget and Advanced Materials (Vehicle Architecture) 14 July 2026 (Day 4)
monitoring as well as structural health monitoring, measuring strain, temperature, pressure-related
structural response, and vibration [11]. Recent reusable-launch-vehicle landing-system research also
reports that FBG sensors can provide strain-monitoring performance comparable to conventional strain
gauges while offering reliability advantages under impact conditions [12].
For the baseline, the reuse-passport architecture concentrates sensors at the catch ring and catch-lug
backing frames, where peak capture strain and asymmetric engagement loads are expected; at the
interstage and grid-fin roots, where descent-control and bending loads are introduced; at the thrust
structure and engine bay, where ascent thrust loads and thermal cycles are concentrated; at selected
tank barrel and dome regions, where cryogenic pressure cycles and local deformation are monitored; and
at selective TPS zones, where temperature sensors determine whether reusable protection patches
exceeded inspection thresholds.
The reuse passport is an architectural feature rather than a massless software addition. It requires sensor
fibers, interrogators, routing protection, data handling, and inspection criteria. The mass is expected to be
small relative to the total vehicle mass, but it is included conceptually in avionics and recovery-system
allowances in the later mass table. The main value is operational: by quantifying actual flight loads, the
system reduces unnecessary refurbishment and identifies components that require replacement before
reflight.

3.3 Catch Ring and Load Paths
The key structural innovation in the baseline concept is a hybrid catch-compatible first stage. Instead of
carrying full Falcon-style landing legs as the primary support system, the booster carries a
circumferential catch ring, four primary catch-lug assemblies, and small emergency crush pads. The
recovery vessel carries the major energy-absorbing net/cable system and hydraulic damping hardware.
This separates flying recovery mass from non-flying recovery infrastructure.
The catch ring is not simply an external attachment band. It must distribute concentrated capture loads
into the cylindrical tank structure without creating local buckling, tearing, or permanent deformation. The
proposed load path is:
catch cable → replaceable wear shoe → catch lug → catch ring → ring frames/stringers → tank barrel
and interstage structure.
The catch lug is defined as a modular interface. The main lug body may use titanium alloy or maraging
steel, while the contact surface uses a replaceable wear shoe made from a tougher, sacrificial material
such as coated steel, Inconel, or a ceramic-coated insert. The replaceable wear shoe prevents the most
damaged surface from being part of the permanent booster structure. This is important because the
catch event will involve sliding, local compression, dynamic cable contact, and possible thermal
exposure from the landing burn.

Local tank reinforcement avoids simply thickening the full booster shell. A more mass-efficient concept is
local orthogrid or isogrid reinforcement around the catch-ring belt. NASA technical work on isogrid
structures states that isogrid concepts have been efficient and practical in launch-vehicle nose fairings,
adapters, cylindrical propellant tank sections, and Skylab panels, with high strength-to-mass potential
[13]. More general launch-vehicle structural references also describe integrally machined isogrid or
orthogrid stiffeners as common methods for stiffened metal propellant tanks [9]. In the present design,
the catch belt would use local grid stiffening only where recovery loads enter the structure, rather than
applying heavy reinforcement over the full first-stage barrel.
This concept is novel within the project because it connects recovery architecture directly to the mass
budget. The mass of catch lugs, catch ring, local reinforcement, sensors, and emergency pads will be
grouped under first-stage recovery hardware and local structure in the later mass budget. The recovery
vessel’s cable frame, dampers, and clamps remain non-flying infrastructure and are therefore not
counted in GLOM.
3.4 Tank Structure
The baseline tank material remains aluminum-lithium alloy, because it provides a favorable combination
of strength-to-weight ratio, cryogenic compatibility, weldability, and existing launch-vehicle heritage.
SpaceX identifies Falcon 9 first-stage tanks as aluminum-lithium alloy structures manufactured using
friction stir welding [2]. Industry history on aluminum-lithium alloys reports that alloy 2195 replaced legacy
2219 in the Space Shuttle Super Lightweight External Tank and provided more than 6,000 lb of weight
savings, with lower density and improved strength/modulus characteristics [14]. The same source
describes friction stir welding as a major enabler for Al-Li cryogenic tank fabrication because it reduces
weld-property knockdowns compared with more conventional fusion-welding approaches [14].
For this project, the metallic baseline is preferred because the tanks are primary flight structures that
must tolerate cryogenic cycles, axial compression, bending, and recovery-induced loads. Metallic tanks
also offer mature non-destructive inspection methods, direct monitoring of weld regions, and a more
straightforward path for distributing concentrated catch loads through ring frames and stringers than
large composite tank laminates.
Composite cryogenic tanks remain a future option, especially for the upper stage where dry-mass
savings are highly valuable. NASA’s Composite Cryotank Technology Demonstration targeted tanks more
than 30% lighter and 25% lower cost than comparable metallic cryogenic tanks [15]. However, large
composite cryotanks have historically faced microcracking, permeation, and cryopumping challenges;
NASA/Boeing composite cryotank reporting discusses earlier X-33 tank delamination and cryopumping
issues while noting that the results did not invalidate composite tanks as a technology [16]. Therefore, the
baseline does not adopt full composite booster tanks. Instead, it reserves composite cryotanks as an
upper-stage stretch option for later optimization.
A more practical near-term composite use is in non-pressure structures: payload fairing, interstage
panels, avionics access covers, and possibly removable thermal-protection support panels.
Thermoplastic composites may be considered for these secondary structures because NASA’s review of
thermoplastic composite advances highlights in-situ consolidation, automated fiber placement, and

fastener-less welding as promising manufacturing approaches [17]. This is relevant to refurbishment:
removable or weld-repairable composite panels could reduce turnaround time compared with bonded
thermoset repairs.
3.5 Selective Thermal Protection
The booster does not require full-body orbital-entry thermal protection, but it does require local
protection against reentry heating, plume recirculation, and landing-burn thermal exposure. A full-body
tile system would add unnecessary mass and inspection burden. The selected strategy is therefore
selective reusable TPS.
Reusable TPS literature distinguishes ablative systems, which dissipate heat by material consumption,
from reusable insulation systems that remain structurally intact through heating cycles. NASA reusable
TPS material summaries identify Shuttle tile, AFRSI blanket, and TUFROC as reusable TPS families [18]. A
2024 reusable TPS study discusses AETB tiles, TUFI coatings, TUFROC, ceramic matrix composites, and
other reusable reentry materials; it notes that reusable TPS manages heat through low conductivity, high
emissivity, and thermal isolation rather than by being consumed [19]. NASA reports that Advanced
TUFROC has demonstrated repeated exposure capability under high-temperature arc-jet testing and is a
state-of-the-art reusable TPS for high-temperature applications [20].
The booster uses TPS only at locations where the expected heat flux or thermal-cycle damage is
structurally important. These locations include the engine bay and octaweb perimeter, the base
heat-shield region around engine plumbing and actuators, grid-fin leading edges and root attachments,
catch lugs and catch-ring external surfaces, exposed raceways and feedline fairings, and selected
interstage or aft-tank zones exposed during reentry attitude changes.
This approach creates three advantages. First, it minimizes TPS mass. Second, it reduces inspection area
after recovery. Third, it allows modular replacement of high-wear thermal panels. The selected TPS family
is not finalized at this stage; instead, the architecture allocates a protected thermal zone and reserves
later selection among ceramic blanket, TUFROC-class panels, coated metallic panels, and limited
ablative patches. A useful design rule for the later mass budget is that TPS is treated as a local
subsystem, not as a generic airframe mass addition.

3.6 Additive Manufacturing for Hot Structures
Additive manufacturing is not used as a general claim that the rocket is “3D printed.” Its value is highest
where geometry is complex, part count is high, cooling channels are integrated, or local
high-temperature performance is required. The architecture therefore uses additive manufacturing
selectively.
NASA’s one-piece liquid rocket thrust chamber assembly concept uses additively manufactured
GRCop42 or GRCop84 copper-alloy chamber technology, bimetallic transitions, and composite overwrap;
NASA states that replacing a traditional metal jacket with composite overwrap can reduce overall thrust
chamber assembly weight by over 40% [21]. NASA’s RAMPT-related additive manufacturing work also

emphasizes part-count reduction, integrated coolant channels, and shorter fabrication lead times for
large regeneratively cooled engine hardware [21]. Separately, NASA’s GRX-810 alloy is a 3D-printable
high-temperature alloy developed for rocket-engine and high-heat aerospace environments, with
improved durability under elevated temperature conditions [22].
The architecture applies these developments only in constrained areas where the benefit is clear. Engine
manifolds and injector-adjacent parts can use additive manufacturing to reduce joints and simplify
internal flow paths. Regeneratively cooled chamber or nozzle subcomponents can use GRCop-class
copper alloys in high heat-flux regions, while superalloys or GRX-810-class materials can support hotter

distribute concentrated loads while remaining inspectable. Replaceable hot-side panels can use modular
printed or machined Inconel or superalloy hardware to protect the engine bay after high-heating flights.
This approach maintains engineering discipline: the baseline still uses a Merlin-class engine family
selected in the propulsion trade study, and this report does not redesign the engine cycle. Additive
manufacturing is introduced as a materials and manufacturability enhancement for high-value
subcomponents, not as a wholesale propulsion-system change.
3.7 Recovery Interface Materials
The hybrid catch architecture requires both flying and non-flying recovery materials. The booster-side
hardware must be stiff, damage tolerant, heat tolerant, and inspectable. The ship-side net/cable system
must absorb energy while resisting marine degradation and repeated dynamic loading.
Public reporting on Long March 10B confirms the use of a sea-based net/cable recovery system, but the
exact fiber composition of the net has not been officially released. Industry analysis has suggested
ultra-high-molecular-weight polyethylene (UHMWPE) and aramid-type fibers as plausible candidate
material families because UHMWPE provides very high specific strength and marine resistance, while
aramids provide better heat tolerance [23]. This report treats that material combination as an engineering
candidate, not as confirmed Long March 10B data.

3.8 Propellant Slosh Control
First-stage recovery differs from ascent because the booster returns with relatively low residual
propellant mass. During reorientation, entry burn, descent, and final landing burn, residual LOX and RP-1
can slosh, shifting the center of mass and affecting engine inlet conditions. This is especially important for
a catch-compatible system because final position and attitude errors directly affect catch loads.
Cryogenic propellant-management literature identifies baffles and bladders as passive slosh-mitigation
devices, while noting that additional internal structures increase system mass [24]. A classic NASA study
on flexible baffles investigated lightweight baffles for slosh suppression and argued that flexible baffles
could potentially provide high damping per unit weight compared with more massive conventional
baffles [25].
The architecture therefore includes low-mass propellant-management hardware as a deliberate design
feature rather than an untracked internal detail. The preliminary approach combines perforated or
lightweight baffles in the first-stage tanks, anti-vortex and propellant-management devices near engine
feed outlets, sensor correlation between tank pressure and acceleration data, and later trajectory
coupling between residual propellant level and final descent control.
The mass of slosh-control hardware is expected to be modest relative to tank and propellant mass, but it
is included conceptually in the stage dry-mass allocation. The key architectural point is that recovery
reliability is not determined only by engines and grid fins; it also depends on whether residual propellant
remains feedable and dynamically controlled during final burns.

4.3 Stage Functional Layout
The architecture uses a conventional two-stage division but assigns clear functional responsibilities to
each stage. The first stage supplies high thrust through the dense lower atmosphere and survives the
recovery environment. The second stage completes orbital insertion and provides precise payload
deployment. This distribution is consistent with Falcon 9’s general architecture, where the first stage uses
nine sea-level engines and the second stage uses a single vacuum engine [1], [2].
4.3.1 First Stage
The first stage is the reusable asset and carries the majority of the propulsion hardware. It provides
lift-off and early-ascent thrust, carries the largest propellant load, separates cleanly from the upper
stage, reorients after separation, executes entry and landing/catch burns, controls atmospheric
descent through grid fins and RCS, interfaces with the ocean recovery system, and preserves structural
integrity for inspection and reflight.
The propulsion arrangement follows a nine-engine layout with eight outer engines surrounding a
central engine. The Falcon User’s Guide describes this arrangement for Falcon 9 M1D engines and
states that the first-stage total thrust is 7,605 kN using nine engines at 845 kN each at sea level [2]. The
central-engine position is retained because it is the most suitable engine for final descent control,
deep-throttle landing logic, and symmetric thrust during the terminal recovery phase.
The first stage also carries the recovery-specific structures identified in Section 3: grid fins, RCS,
selective TPS, catch ring, catch lugs, sensor network, and emergency crush pads. Unlike a purely
legged booster, the major recovery energy absorber is not carried entirely on the vehicle. This reduces
the flying recovery mass but introduces a new structural requirement: the catch loads must be
transmitted into the booster without damaging the tank shell or interstage structure.
4.3.2 Second Stage
The second stage is expendable in the baseline configuration. It ignites after stage separation, provides
vacuum-optimized propulsion, completes orbital insertion into the SSO mission target, executes

===== PAGE 46 =====

restart-capable burns when required by mission design, maintains attitude control during coast and
payload deployment, and deploys the payload through a low-shock separation interface.
The single-engine upper-stage configuration is retained because it minimizes engine count and dry
mass while providing sufficient vacuum operation. Falcon 9’s second stage uses a single Merlin Vacuum
engine, which SpaceX identifies as restartable and capable of delivering payloads to the desired orbit
[1]. The Falcon User’s Guide further describes the second-stage tank as a shorter version of the
first-stage tank using similar materials, construction, tooling, and manufacturing techniques [2]. This
supports a low-development-risk baseline: the upper stage uses the same propellant family and
related tank manufacturing approach as the booster.
The baseline does not add upper-stage recovery hardware. Full upper-stage reuse would require a
heat shield, reentry control, landing system, and additional propellant reserve, all of which would
reduce payload capability. That trade is not excluded permanently, but it is not included in the
preliminary architecture because the mission already demands a 20,000 kg payload to SSO.

4.6 Vehicle Interfaces and Separation Systems
The architecture uses deliberately simple stage and payload separation interfaces. The Falcon User’s
Guide states that Falcon’s interstage houses pneumatic pushers for stage separation and that Falcon
systems use low-shock pneumatic devices rather than pyrotechnic separation for certain separation
functions [2]. This motivates a low-shock separation philosophy for the present vehicle, especially
because reusable structures benefit from repeatable, inspectable separation hardware.
The main interfaces are:
1. Payload-to-adapter interface. This interface transfers payload loads during ascent and provides
low-shock payload separation. It also defines the mechanical boundary for payload mass and
center of gravity.
2. Fairing separation interface. The fairing is jettisoned after the dense-atmosphere ascent phase.
Falcon 9 fairings are carbon-composite and recovered by SpaceX for reuse [1], making fairing
recovery a possible cost feature, but not a primary performance driver in the present architecture.
3. Second-stage-to-interstage interface. This interface carries upper-stage loads during ascent and
releases the second stage after first-stage cutoff. A pneumatic pusher or similarly low-shock
mechanism is preferred for testability and reduced debris generation.
4. Interstage-to-first-stage tank interface. This interface must carry ascent bending loads,
stage-separation loads, grid-fin loads, and part of the catch-ring load path.
5. Tank-to-thrust-structure interface. This region transfers engine thrust into the tank barrel and
must also tolerate landing/catch burn loads.
6. Catch-ring-to-booster interface. This is a new interface introduced by the selected architecture. It
requires local reinforcement, sensors, and replaceable wear features.
Interface design is treated as a mass-budget driver. Each interface adds structure, fasteners, separation
hardware, sensors, routing, and inspection access. The next section therefore treats interstage,
separation, catch hardware, and recovery systems as explicit mass categories rather than hidden
structural margin.

4.7 Recovery Operations Sequence
The recovery sequence defines the required hardware and directly informs mass allocation. The baseline
sequence is:
1. Main engine cutoff and stage separation. The first stage separates after completing the booster
ascent phase. The second stage continues toward orbit.
2. Attitude reorientation. RCS and aerodynamic surfaces orient the booster for entry. The booster
must maintain propellant settling adequate for relight.
3. Entry burn. A subset of engines reduces reentry energy and heating. The exact number of
engines and burn duration are trajectory-dependent.
4. Atmospheric descent. Grid fins provide lift/drag modulation and cross-range correction. The
catch-compatible architecture still requires accurate terminal positioning.
5. Terminal burn. The center engine, or a multi-engine-to-single-engine sequence, reduces vertical
velocity and aligns the booster with the vessel capture frame.
6. Cable/net engagement. The catch lugs engage the recovery system. The vessel-side cable
frame and dampers absorb the residual kinetic energy.
7. Securing and safing. The booster is clamped, safed, and stabilized for transport. Propellant
residuals and pressurants are handled according to recovery safety procedures.
8. Post-flight inspection. Sensor data, thermal exposure, catch-lug wear, grid-fin condition,
engine-bay TPS, and tank strain history are uploaded to the reuse passport.
This sequence differs from a conventional drone-ship landing in the last two phases: touchdown is
replaced by catch engagement and vessel-side damping. The propulsion, guidance, and aerodynamic
phases remain similar enough to be supported by Falcon-derived operational logic, while the terminal
structural interface is closer to the Long March 10B recovery concept.

5. Mass Budget
5.1 Budgeting Method
The mass budget is a first-order closure model for the architecture defined in Section 4. It is not a
trajectory-optimized sizing result. Its purpose is to verify whether the selected architecture can be
represented by a physically coherent mass allocation at the approximate 600,000 kg gross lift-off mass
target. The budget separates payload, propellant, stage dry hardware, recovery systems, vehicle-level
avionics, and design margin so that later trajectory and optimization work can modify each category
independently.
The following accounting rules are used:
1. Propellant and dry hardware are separated. LOX/RP-1 propellant is not included in engine, tank, or
structural dry mass.
2. Complete engine mass is not double-counted. Engine allowances include chamber, turbopump,
nozzle, valves directly attached to the engine, and thrust-vector-control hardware at the level
required for preliminary sizing.
3. Recovery hardware is explicit. Grid fins, catch lugs, catch ring, selective TPS, RCS, landing/catch
sensors, and emergency crush pads are not hidden inside generic airframe mass.
4. Non-flying recovery infrastructure is excluded from GLOM. The recovery vessel, cable/net frame,
hydraulic dampers, deck clamps, and handling equipment are treated as ground/maritime
infrastructure, not flight mass.
5. All non-sourced values are estimates. Estimated values are derived by Falcon-class scaling,
architecture closure, or recovery-system trade assumptions, as identified in the tables below.

5.5 Recovery Hardware and Propellant Allocation
The recovery system is the distinguishing mass trade in this architecture. In a legged landing architecture,
the booster must carry deployable legs, leg actuators, locking mechanisms, impact-absorbing structures,
and local reinforcements. In the hybrid catch-compatible architecture, part of that function is transferred
to the vessel-side cable/net and damping system. This shift reduces flight mass but increases
dependence on recovery infrastructure.
Reusable-launch-vehicle optimization literature often treats landing gear and grid fins as explicit
first-stage dry-mass penalties. One AIAA multidisciplinary optimization study applies a 15% increase to
first-stage dry mass to represent landing gear such as legs and grid fins, based on reverse engineering of
Falcon 9, and applies additional dry-mass margins of 10% for the upper stage and 15% for the first stage
[26]. The present budget does not directly apply those exact percentages; instead, it uses them as a
reasonableness reference for explicit recovery-mass accounting.

5.8 Budget Uncertainty and Handoff
The mass budget closes numerically, but several categories carry substantial uncertainty. The most
important are:
1. First-stage recovery propellant reserve. The 18,000 kg value is estimated and depends on staging
velocity, entry burn, downrange distance, terminal burn profile, and catch-system tolerance.
2. Catch-ring and catch-lug mass. The 5,500 kg recovery-hardware allocation includes several new
components whose mass must be verified by structural load analysis.
3. Second-stage propellant mass. The 112,000 kg value is a Falcon-class scaling estimate. It must be
tested against the required SSO insertion energy.
4. Dry-mass growth margin. The 7,000 kg margin is adequate for a first-order concept but may be
consumed quickly if catch loads require heavier reinforcement.
5. TPS mass. Selective TPS is assumed, but thermal analysis may expand or reduce the protected
area.
6. Slosh-control and residual-propellant management. These systems are included conceptually but
require more detailed tank and recovery-burn modeling.
The mass budget therefore supports the selected architecture but does not yet prove mission closure. It
defines the numerical baseline for the subsequent performance and verification calculations:
thrust-to-weight ratio, propellant mass fraction, stage wet/dry ratios, recovery reserve adequacy, and
sensitivity to dry-mass growth. The next section uses these mass allocations to perform first-order
consistency checks without replacing the later trajectory simulation.

6.5 Sensitivity Logic
The same mass dictionary can be used for first-order sensitivity checks. The most important near-term
sensitivity is dry-mass growth in the recovery system. Because the total GLOM is fixed, any growth in

The primary tanks use aluminum-lithium alloy with friction stir welded construction. This selection is
supported by Falcon 9’s published tank architecture: SpaceX states that Falcon 9 uses aluminum-lithium
alloy tanks containing LOX and RP-1 [1], and the Falcon User’s Guide states that Falcon tank walls are
aluminum-lithium alloy manufactured using friction stir welding [2]. Aluminum-lithium 2195 heritage is
also relevant: industry reporting states that alloy 2195 replaced legacy 2219 in the Space Shuttle Super

For the present vehicle, aluminum-lithium is selected for both stages because it provides high
strength-to-weight performance relative to conventional aluminum tank alloys, established cryogenic
structural heritage, compatibility with friction stir welding, predictable inspection using established
metallic non-destructive evaluation methods, and direct load-path compatibility with ring frames,
stringers, thrust structures, and catch-ring reinforcement.
The principal disadvantages are weld-process sensitivity, local buckling risk, and limited
high-temperature capability. These disadvantages are mitigated by keeping tanks away from direct
hot-flow exposure, applying selective TPS near the engine bay and catch ring, and using local
isogrid/orthogrid reinforcement where concentrated loads enter the tank shell. Composite cryotanks are
not adopted as the baseline because large composite cryogenic tanks still carry microcracking,
permeation, cryopumping, and inspection risks, even though NASA composite cryotank work has
targeted more than 30% weight reduction and 25% cost reduction relative to metallic cryotanks [15], [16].
7.3 Composite Fairing and Interstage
Composite materials are applied where stiffness and low mass are required without primary cryogenic
pressure containment. The payload fairing uses a carbon-composite sandwich structure. SpaceX states
that the Falcon 9 fairing is made of carbon-composite material and is recovered for reuse on future
missions [1]. This provides a credible reference for a 5.2 m class fairing envelope.
The interstage uses carbon-fiber face sheets with an aluminum honeycomb core. The Falcon User’s
Guide describes the Falcon interstage as a composite structure consisting of an aluminum honeycomb
core surrounded by carbon-fiber face-sheet plies [2]. For the present architecture, the interstage has
expanded functional importance because it is close to the grid-fin region and the catch-compatible load
path. It must support axial compression and bending during ascent, second-stage loads before
separation, stage-separation loads, local grid-fin loads, protected sensor routing, and catch-ring load
transfer into the first-stage tank structure.
Thermoplastic composites are retained as an advanced manufacturing option for removable interstage
covers, access panels, fairing substructures, and repairable secondary panels. NASA’s review of
thermoplastic composites identifies in-situ consolidation, automated fiber placement, and fastener-less
welding as important advances for future composite structures [17]. Their advantages are rapid
processing and weldable repair; their disadvantages are material qualification, thermal-process control,
and limited launch-vehicle heritage relative to conventional thermoset composite sandwich structures.
7.4 Thrust Structure and Engine-Bay Materials
The thrust structure transfers engine loads into the first-stage tank and is therefore part of the primary
structural load path. The baseline uses an aluminum-lithium structural frame with local high-temperature
and high-load fittings. Metallic continuity between the tank and thrust frame simplifies load transfer and
inspection. However, local hot and high-stress regions require superalloy or titanium reinforcement.

7.7 Sensor and Inspection Integration
The material system is integrated with the reuse-passport concept. FBG sensors are embedded or
surface-mounted along the catch ring, interstage, grid-fin roots, tank wall, and thrust structure. ESA
identifies FBG sensors as promising for reusable cryogenic tank and intertank monitoring because they
can be multiplexed and embedded or surface mounted [10]. Composite propellant-tank research further
describes embedded FBG networks as useful for structural and operational monitoring of strain,
temperature, and vibration [11].
The sensor system is assembled through protected fiber routes along ring frames and non-penetrating
external raceways where possible. Lug backing plates and catch-ring segments include strain sensors
and replaceable local thermal sensors, while the engine bay uses thermocouples and fiber sensors to
track thermal exposure near hot panels and TPS boundaries. Post-flight data are then compared with
allowable strain, thermal exposure, and catch-load thresholds.
The sensor network does not remove the need for physical inspection. It reduces uncertainty by
identifying where inspection is most necessary. This supports a more efficient refurbishment flow and
prevents the recovery architecture from relying only on visual checks after a high-energy catch event.
7.8 Structural Assembly Concept
The integrated structural stack is assembled through a combination of welded metallic pressure
structures, bolted or bonded composite secondary structures, and modular replaceable recovery
hardware.
The assembly concept begins with aluminum-lithium panels friction stir welded into cylindrical tank
sections, domes, and common-dome or close-coupled bulkhead assemblies. Orthogrid or isogrid
reinforcement is applied locally in the catch-ring belt, thrust-transfer region, and selected interface zones
rather than across the entire tank. The carbon-fiber/honeycomb interstage attaches to metallic tank
interfaces through bolted flanges and load-spreading rings, while the carbon-composite fairing halves
attach to the payload module and separate after the dense-atmosphere phase. The engine support
frame attaches to the aft tank region and carries local Inconel or titanium hot fittings. Catch lugs, wear
shoes, grid fins, TPS covers, and crush pads are installed as inspectable and replaceable modules, and

metallic and inspectable; composite materials reduce mass where pressure containment is not required;
hot and high-wear components are localized and replaceable; and recovery-critical structures are
instrumented for reflight certification. The material architecture therefore directly supports the mass
budget and prepares the vehicle for recovery-system verification.
8. Recovery Architecture
8.1 Recovery Objectives and Mode Selection
The recovery system is treated as an integrated extension of the launch vehicle rather than a post-flight
handling process. Its objective is to return the first stage intact, above the ocean surface, structurally
inspectable, and suitable for later reflight assessment. The baseline uses downrange ocean recovery
because the SSO mission class implies a high-inclination ascent corridor and because
return-to-launch-site recovery would require additional boostback propellant. Mission-performance
studies of vertical-landing reusable launch vehicles show that downrange landing can be more
propellant-efficient and more robust than return-to-launch-site recovery over broad main-engine-cutoff
conditions [32]. RETALT mission-engineering work similarly treats downrange landing on an ocean barge
as a major recovery mode for retropropulsive stages and emphasizes that propellant budget, peak entry
conditions, and landing loads must be evaluated together [33].
Three recovery modes were considered: conventional legged propulsive landing, pure cable/net catch,
and hybrid catch-compatible recovery. A legged drone-ship landing has the strongest operational
precedent because Falcon 9 routinely lands on ocean platforms [5], [6], but it requires the booster to carry
deployable legs, actuators, locks, and leg-load reinforcement throughout ascent. A pure catch concept
has the lowest onboard support mass, but it concentrates recovery success into a single terminal
engagement event and has limited public operational history beyond the reported Long March 10B
demonstration [3]. The selected hybrid architecture retains retropropulsive descent, grid-fin steering, RCS
attitude control, and sensor-assisted terminal guidance, while replacing full landing legs with a catch ring,
four replaceable catch-lug assemblies, and limited emergency crush pads.

The recovery vessel is a non-flying subsystem, but it strongly influences booster design. It provides
station keeping, a capture frame, controlled cable compliance, hydraulic or mechanical damping,
alignment beacons, blast-resistant deck protection, post-catch clamps, and safing equipment. Since

8.4 Thermal, Safing, and Reuse Certification
The terminal recovery phase creates coupled thermal and operational hazards. The final burn occurs near
the recovery vessel, so plume heating, acoustic loading, and hot-gas impingement affect both booster
and vessel hardware. The booster-side thermal-protection system is therefore concentrated at the aft
engine-bay panels, the lower tank-dome region, the cable-facing catch-lug covers, the grid-fin root
thermal shields, and exposed sensor or wiring raceways. The vessel-side system requires blast-resistant
deck surfaces, protected cable-frame components, and routing that keeps sensitive equipment outside
direct plume exposure.
The baseline assumes a short terminal burn rather than sustained hover. This is consistent with a
mass-efficient retropropulsive recovery concept but remains a trajectory and controls assumption. A
longer burn would increase both propellant demand and local thermal exposure, requiring larger TPS
coverage and more robust vessel shielding. The thermal design is therefore linked directly to the
recovery reserve and terminal guidance solution.

This post-catch process is essential to the economic value of reuse. Sensor-based certification does not

thermal exceedances. The architecture therefore treats recovery, safing, inspection, and refurbishment as
a single closed loop.

The recovery architecture creates three verification priorities for subsequent analysis. The first is trajectory
verification: the recovery reserve must be tested against actual staging velocity, entry burn, terminal burn,
and downrange vessel location. The second is structural verification: the catch ring, catch lugs, local
reinforcement, and wear shoes must be sized against asymmetric engagement and dynamic cable
loading. The third is operational verification: vessel motion, sensor fusion, plume exposure, and
post-catch safing must be modeled as part of the recovery system rather than external operations.
In summary, the recovery architecture defines the distinctive character of the vehicle. It preserves the
known advantages of retropropulsive descent and grid-fin control while shifting part of the final support
and energy absorption into non-flying infrastructure. The concept reduces flight mass relative to a legged
fallback, but it requires rigorous verification of terminal guidance, catch dynamics, vessel infrastructure,
and reuse certification.
9. Conclusion
The completed architecture defines a coherent 600,000 kg class reusable launch vehicle for a 20,000 kg
payload to SSO. The configuration retains the proven advantages of a two-stage LOX/RP-1 system with a
nine-engine reusable booster and a single-engine expendable upper stage, while introducing a hybrid
ocean catch-compatible recovery architecture. This recovery concept reduces the flight mass associated
with full landing legs by shifting part of the terminal energy absorption to a vessel-mounted cable/net
and damping system. The resulting design remains conservative in propulsion and tank technology but
exploratory in recovery integration, where the catch ring, replaceable catch lugs, selective TPS, and
reuse-passport sensor network form the principal distinguishing features.
The preliminary mass budget closes at 600,000 kg with 521,000 kg of propellant, a payload fraction of
3.33%, a total propellant mass fraction of 86.83%, and a flying recovery penalty of 3.92%. Using nine
Merlin-class sea-level engines at 845 kN each, based on Falcon User’s Guide data [2], the lift-off
thrust-to-weight ratio is approximately 1.29. These values indicate first-order consistency between the
mass target and propulsion architecture, although they do not yet demonstrate orbital performance. The
18,000 kg recovery propellant reserve, the 5,500 kg recovery-hardware allocation, and the 7,000 kg
dry-mass growth margin are explicitly identified as estimates. Their adequacy depends on the later
recovery-trajectory, structural-load, and thermal analyses.
The material strategy is deliberately zoned rather than uniformly exotic. Aluminum-lithium tanks and
friction stir welded metallic pressure structures provide the baseline load-bearing architecture;

===== PAGE 74 =====
Mass Budget and Advanced Materials (Vehicle Architecture) 14 July 2026 (Day 4)
carbon-composite structures are assigned to the fairing and interstage; titanium, Inconel, GRCop-class
copper alloys, and TUFROC-class reusable TPS are reserved for localized high-load or high-temperature
regions. This allocation supports inspection and refurbishment by keeping high-wear components
modular and replaceable.
The principal unresolved technical questions are the terminal catch dynamics, sea-state tolerance,
recovery propellant reserve, catch-ring structural sizing, selective TPS coverage, and upper-stage
propellant adequacy for SSO insertion. These issues are not deficiencies in the architecture; they are the
verification targets created by the architecture. Consistent with formal verification and validation practice,
subsequent work converts the assumptions in this report into trajectory, structural, thermal, and
operational requirements that can be tested by analysis, simulation, or inspection [36]. The resulting
baseline is therefore suitable for the next project phase: quantitative trajectory and recovery-load
verification.

Aerodynamics
1.1. What is Aerodynamics?
Aerodynamics is the study of how air flows around a moving object and the forces that result from this
interaction. For a rocket, aerodynamics governs the drag, lift, and pitching moments that act on the
vehicle during atmospheric flight. Aerodynamic forces significantly affect the rocket's trajectory, structural
loads, and stability. Understanding these forces is essential for predicting flight performance, ensuring
structural integrity, and optimizing the ascent path [1].

1.4 Reentry Aerodynamics
For our reusable first stage, the reentry phase presents unique aerodynamic challenges. After stage
separation, the first stage coasts to apogee before descending through the atmosphere at hypersonic
speeds (Mach 5–10). The key phases are:
● Entry Burn – The engine fires briefly to slow the stage before it hits the dense atmosphere,

● Aerodynamic Deceleration – Drag slows the stage from supersonic to subsonic speeds. Grid fins

● Landing Burn – In the final seconds, the engine fires again to slow the stage to ~2 m/s for

Trajectory
2.1. What is Trajectory?
A trajectory is the path that a rocket follows through space and time, from liftoff on the launch pad to
the final orbital insertion. It is defined by the rocket's position, velocity, and acceleration at every moment
during flight. The trajectory is not a fixed, predetermined line—it is the result of careful engineering
decisions that balance performance, structural loads, and mission constraints [8].
[8]: (https://www.grc.nasa.gov/WWW/K-12/rocket/launch.html)
2.2. Rocket Trajectory Fundamentals
The ascent trajectory is a carefully calculated balance between achieving the required velocity and
minimizing losses [5].
A typical rocket ascent follows these phases:
● Vertical Ascent – Immediately after liftoff, the rocket flies vertically to clear the launch tower. This
phase lasts only a few seconds.
● Gravity Turn – The rocket gradually pitches over from vertical toward horizontal. The pitch
program is designed so that the velocity vector aligns with the local horizontal as altitude
increases. This minimizes aerodynamic loads and steering losses. The trajectory optimizes
between gravity losses and atmospheric drag losses: reaching orbital velocity as soon as possible
reduces gravity losses but increases drag at low altitudes [6].
● Max-Q – The point of maximum dynamic pressure. At Max-Q, the rocket experiences the highest
aerodynamic loads. This typically occurs at 10–15 km altitude [3].
● Staging – After the first stage burns out, it separates from the upper stage at approximately
60–100 km altitude, where atmospheric density is low enough for efficient upper-stage operation.
● Second Stage Ascent – The upper stage continues the ascent, gradually pitching to a
near-horizontal trajectory as it approaches orbital velocity [7].

2.3. The ΔVBudget and Trajectory Losses
The total ΔV required to reach orbit is not just the orbital velocity. The rocket must also overcome several
losses during ascent. Our mission requires approximately 11.0 km/s of total ΔV to reach SSO.
Orbital Velocity – For a 500 km SSO, the required orbital velocity is approximately 7.6 km/s. However, the
rocket must provide significantly more ΔV due to losses.
Gravity Losses – These occur because the rocket fights Earth's gravitational pull during ascent. Longer
vertical ascent means greater gravity losses. For a typical two-stage rocket, gravity losses are
approximately 1.0–1.5 km/s.
Drag Losses – These occur because the rocket must push through the atmosphere. Typical drag losses
are approximately 0.2–0.5 km/s.
Steering Losses – These occur when the thrust vector is not perfectly aligned with the velocity vector
during the gravity turn. These are typically small (~0.1 km/s) [8].
2.4. Ascent Trajectory Optimization and Constraints
Trajectory optimization is the process of determining the optimal control history and flight parameters
that maximize performance while satisfying all mission constraints. For a multistage launch vehicle, the
ascent trajectory optimization problem aims to maximize payload mass injected into the target orbit .
Optimization Objectives:
● Maximize payload mass – the primary objective for a launch vehicle
● Minimize propellant consumption – achieved by optimizing thrust direction, staging times, and
coast durations
● Minimize trajectory losses – reducing gravity, drag, and steering losses through trajectory shaping
Key Decision Variables:
● Thrust direction time history (pitch and yaw angles)
● Staging times and coast durations
● Kick-off angle and initial pitch-over maneuver timing
● Third-stage burn duration and orientation
Path Constraints (Must be satisfied during flight):
● Maximum dynamic pressure (Max-Q) – structural load limit, typically 25–35 kPa
● Maximum axial acceleration – limits structural and payload loads (typically <5–6 g)
● Angle of attack limits – prevents excessive side loads and aerodynamic instability
● Heat flux limits – thermal protection system constraints (relevant for reentry)

Reusability Strategy
1.1. What is Reusability?
Reusability in rocketry refers to the ability to recover and refly major components of a launch vehicle after
a mission. This stands in contrast to expendable launch vehicles (ELVs), where stages are discarded after
a single use . The primary goal of reusability is to reduce the cost of space access by amortizing the
manufacturing cost of expensive hardware over multiple flights .
For our rocket, the first stage is the largest and most expensive component – accounting for
approximately 60% of a rocket's total cost . By recovering and reusing the first stage, we can achieve
significant cost reductions over the vehicle's operational lifetime. Reusable launchers with reasonable
parameters can significantly reduce the cost of expendable launchers . SpaceX has demonstrated that
recovering and reusing boosters can cut launch costs by more than 70 percent, with Falcon 9 pricing
reaching around $3,000 per kilogram .
1.2. Recovery Methods (Overview)
There are three primary recovery methods for reusable first stages :
1. Vertical Takeoff, Vertical Landing (VTVL)
● The stage lands vertically using rocket propulsion
● Requires engine throttling, multiple restarts, and landing legs
● Demonstrated successfully by SpaceX Falcon 9 and Blue Origin New Shepard
● Suitable for medium-to-large rockets
2. Parachute Recovery
● The stage descends using parachutes (land, sea, or mid-air capture)
● Lower technical requirements on engines – no restart needed
● Mature technology, used on Space Shuttle SRBs and some Chinese rockets
● Lower landing precision; limited to smaller stages
3. Vertical Takeoff, Horizontal Landing (VTHL)
● The stage lands on a runway like an aircraft
● Requires wings, empennage, and landing gear
● Higher maneuverability and cross-range capability
● Lower technology readiness level; higher development cost

After evaluating the three primary recovery approaches, we select Vertical Takeoff, Vertical Landing
